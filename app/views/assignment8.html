General Design Principles



Stop guessing your capacity needs

Eliminate guessing your infrastructure capacity needs; with cloud computing you can scale up and down as needed instead of spending resources needlessly in advance.

Test system at production scale

Outside of the cloud it is usually cost-prohibitive to duplicate your environment for testing purposes, and even if it is, they are not ready for high volumes of traffic. Because the cloud allows you to pay for the environment only when you’re testing, it costs only a fraction of what it would on premises.

Lower the risk of architecture change

Testing environments that emulate your production configuration is an incredibly easy task in the cloud; you can also test serialization that happens in on-premises environments where teams queue the resource tests.

Automate to make architectural experimentation easier

Replicate systems easily and at low cost. Track changes to automation, audit impact and revert to previous states when necessary.

Allow for evolutionary architectures

Architectures are usually implemented as a one time event, with a few major versions of the system in its lifetime. As a business and its context change, these initial decisions may hinder the system’s ability to deliver changing requirements. In the cloud, the capability to automate and test on demand lowers the risk of impact from design.




The Four Pillars of the Well-Architected Framework



As with a building, if the foundations are not solid enough, there might be structural problems that undermine its integrity and function. When architecting technology solutions, if you neglect the four pillars of security, reliability, performance efficiency and cost optimization it can become challenging to build a system that caters to your expectations and requirements.



Security


It encompasses the ability to protect information, systems and assets while delivering the business value through risk assessment and mitigation strategies.

Design Principles

  • Apply security at all layers

  • Enable traceability

  • Automate responses to security events

  • Focus on securing your system

  • Automate best security practices

Major areas

  1. Data protection

  2. Privilege management

  3. Infrastructure protection

  4. Detective controls



Best Practices

1 . Data protection:

  • Customers maintain control over their data.

  • Encrypt data and manage keys through rotation.

  • Logging.

  • Reliability.

  • Versioning.

  • Never move data between regions.

Key Services : Elastic Load Balancing, Amazon Elastic Block Store, S3, Relational Database Service.



2. Privilege management

It is critical to keep root account credentials protected, locking the Multi-Factor Authentication in a physically secured location, as well as managing the Identity and Access Management service correctly in order to establish access levels to resources.

Key Services: IAM, MFA.



3. Infrastructure protection

It is possible to create a private, secured, and scalable environment using the Amazon Virtual Private Cloud, which can help define your topology- including gateways, routing tables, and public and/or private subnets.

It is advisable to implement multiple layers of defense no matter the type of environment: enforcing boundary protection, monitoring points of ingress and egress, and comprehensive logging, monitoring, and alerting are all essential for an effective information security plan.

Key Services: Amazon Virtual Private Cloud (VPC)



4. Detective controls

You can use detective controls to detect or identify a security breach. They are a normal part of governance frameworks, and can be used to support a quality process, a legal compliance obligation, and/or threat identification and response efforts.

Key Services: AWS CloudTrail (which tracks API calls) and AWS Config which provides detailed inventory of your AWS resources and configuration, as well as CloudWatch.




Reliability


The Reliability pillar encompasses the ability of a system to recover from infrastructure or service disruptions, dynamically acquire computing resources to meet demand, and mitigate disruptions such as misconfigurations or transient network issues.

Design Principles

  • Test recovery procedures

  • Automatically recover from failure

  • Scale horizontally to increase aggregate system availability

  • Stop guessing capacity

Definition

  1. Foundations

  2. Change management

  3. Failure management

To achieve reliability, a system must have a well-planned foundation and monitoring in place, with mechanisms for handling changes on demand or requirements. The system should be designed to detect failure and automatically heal itself.



Best Practices


1. Foundations

AWS sets service limits (an upper limit on the number of each resource your team can request) to protect you from accidently over-provisioning resources. You will need to have governance and processes in place to monitor and change these limits to meet your business needs. For all intents and purposes, a hybrid model enables the gradual transition to an all-in, cloud approach over time, and therefore it’s important to have a design for how your AWS and on-premises resources will interact as a network topology.

Key Services: AWS IAM, AWS VPC.



2. Change Management

Being aware of how change affects a system allows you to plan proactively, and monitoring allows you to quickly identify trends that could lead to capacity issues or SLA breaches. Using AWS, you can monitor the behavior of a system and automate the response to KPIs, for example, adding additional servers as a system gains more users. You can control who has permission to make system changes and audit the history of these changes.

With monitoring in place, your team will be automatically alerted when KPIs deviate from expected norms. Automatic logging of changes to your environment allows you to audit and quickly identify actions that might have impacted reliability. Controls on change management ensure that you can enforce the rules that deliver the reliability you need.

Key Services: AWS CloudTrail, AWS Config



3. Failure Management

In any system of reasonable complexity it is expected that failures will occur, and it is generally of interest to know how to become aware of these failures, respond to them, and prevent them from happening again. Since the cloud enables you to stand up temporary versions of a whole system at low cost, you can use automated testing to verify full recovery processes.

Actively track KPIs, such as the recovery time objective (RTO) and recovery point objective (RPO), to assess a system’s fitness (especially under failure-testing scenarios) and to help you identify and mitigate single points of failure.

The objective is to thoroughly test your system-recovery processes so that you are confident that you can recover all your data and continue to serve your customers, even in the face of sustained problems. Your recovery processes should be as well exercised as your normal production processes.

Key Services :

AWS CloudFormation enables template creation of AWS resources and provisions.



Performance Efficiency

Design Principles

  • Democratize advances technologies

  • Go global in minutes

  • Use server-less architectures

  • Experiment more often

Major areas:

  1. Compute

  2. Storage

  3. Database

  4. Space-time trade-off

Considerations:

  1. Select optimal approach and resources

  2. Keep that approach current given evolving capabilities

  3. How to monitor run-time performance against expectations

  4. How the resources scale against demand



Best Practices

Compute

Optimal Server configuration for a particular architecture may vary based on application design, usage patterns, and configuration settings. Selecting the wrong server configuration for a use case can lead to lower performance efficiency. In AWS, servers are virtualized and, therefore, you can change their capabilities with the click of a button or an API call. From an operational standpoint, you should have monitoring in place to notify you of any degradation in performance.

Key Services: Auto scaling is key to ensuring you have enough good instances to meet demand.



Storage

The optimal storage solution for a particular system will vary based on the kind of access method (block, file, or object), patterns of access (random or sequential), throughput required, frequency of access (online, offline, archival), frequency of update (worm, dynamic), and availability and durability constraints. Well architected systems use multiple storage solutions and enable different features to improve performance.

When selecting a storage solution, it is important to have test data that shows which storage solution will deliver the cost/value margin required for that workload. These tests should be repeatable (ideally part of the CD pipeline) so that you can easily test new storage solutions or capabilities as they become available

Key Services: Amazon EBS, S3



Database

The optimal database solution for a particular system can vary based on requirements for consistency, availability, partition tolerance, and latency. Many systems use different database solutions for various sub-systems and enable different features to improve performance. Selecting the wrong database solution and features for a system can lead to lower performance efficiency.

During the build and deployment of your database solution, treat the database as code to allow it to evolve over time rather than be a one-time fixed decision. Test it frequently to identify which database solution matches which workload best. These tests should be repeatable (ideally part of the CD pipeline) so that you can easily test new database solutions or capabilities as they become available.

Key Services: Amazon RDS (Relational Database System), Amazon Dynamo DB for a NoSQL database.




Space-Time trade-off


When architecting solutions, there is a series of trade-offs where space (memory or storage) is used to reduce processing time (compute), or time is used to reduce space. You can also position resources or cached data closer to end users to reduce time.

Use the global infrastructure of AWS to achieve lower latency and higher throughput, and ensure that your data resides only in the region(s) you specify. AWS also offers caching solutions such as Amazon ElastiCache, which helps improve efficiency, and Amazon CloudFront, which caches copies of your static content closer to end-users.

Space-time trade-offs are required to deliver performance efficiency, and it is important to have test data that shows which trade-offs match that workload best.

The architecture should scale with demand and maintain its margin.



Key Services: AWS’ multiple regions which allow you to choose the optimal region for your resources, data and processing. Amazon CloudFront helps with cache location.




Cost Optimization

A cost-optimized system allows you to pay the lowest price possible while still achieving your business objectives and meeting, or exceeding, key requirements for the other Well-Architected pillars. You can achieve cost optimization using techniques to select the appropriate architecture, reduce unused resources, and select the most economical approach.

Design Principles

  • Transparently attribute expenditure

  • Use managed services to reduce cost of ownership

  • Trade capital expense for operating expense

  • Benefit from economies of scale

  • Stop spending money on data center operations

Major areas:

  1. Matched supply and demand

  2. Cost-effective resources

  3. Expenditure awareness

  4. Optimizing over time

In some cases, it’s best to optimize for speed—going to market quickly, shipping new features, or simply meeting a deadline—rather than investing in upfront cost optimization. Design decisions are sometimes guided by haste as opposed to empirical data, as the temptation always exists to overcompensate “just in case” rather than spend time benchmarking for the most cost-optimal deployment. This often leads to drastically over-provisioned and under-optimized deployments.



Best Practices



Matched Supply and Demand

Optimally matching supply to demand delivers the lowest costs for a system, but there also needs to be sufficient extra supply to allow for provisioning time and individual resource failures. If you can anticipate changes in demand, you can save more money and ensure your resources match your system needs. Monitoring tools and regular benchmarking can help you achieve much greater utilization of resources.

Key Services: Auto Scaling to add or remove resources to match demand without overspending.



Cost-Effective Resources

Using the appropriate instances and resources for your system is key to cost savings. A well architected system will use the most cost-effective resources, which can have a significant and positive economic impact. You also have the opportunity to use managed services to reduce costs. For example, rather than maintaining servers to deliver email, you can use a service that charges on a per-message basis.

Key Services: Reserved Instances and prepaid capacity to reduce cost. AWS Trusted Advisor.



Expenditure Awareness

The cloud enables encourages innovation and fast-paced development and deployment. It eliminates the manual processes and time associated with provisioning on-premises infrastructure, the ease of use and virtually unlimited on-demand capacity may require a new way of thinking about expenditures.

Accurate cost attribution also allows you to understand which products are truly profitable, and allows you to make more informed decisions about where to allocate budget.

With this visibility of costs against tagged resources it becomes easier to identify orphaned resources or projects that are no longer generating value to the business and should be decommissioned. You can set up billing alerts to notify you of predicted overspending as well.

Key Services: Amazon CloudWatch, Amazon Simple Notification Service (SNS) will warn you if you go or are forecasted to go over your budgeted amount.



Optimizing over time

As AWS releases new services and features, it is a best practice to reassess your existing architectural decisions to ensure they continue to be the most cost effective. As your requirements change, be aggressive in decommissioning resources and entire services, or systems that you no longer require.

By regularly reassessing your deployment, it is often possible to utilize new AWS services to lower your costs.

Key Services: AWS Blog and What’s New sections on the AWS website, as well as AWS Trusted Advisor for monitoring unused or idle resources committed to a Reserved Instance capacity.
